State of the Indian Plate 2026

We computed IFCT-2017 macros for 1,530 regional dishes across 28 states. Here is what the numbers say about protein, the vegetarian protein gap, and which corner of India eats the most protein per calorie.

By Svas · 25 June 2026 · 6 minute read

Most nutrition conversations in India start with calories. We wanted to start with the food. So we took our IFCT-2017 dataset, the Indian Food Composition Tables from the National Institute of Nutrition, and ran the numbers on 1,530 regional dishes from every corner of the country. Each dish is a real recipe, costed gram by gram, the same way a kitchen actually cooks it.

A few things jumped out. Some confirm what dietitians have said for years. One genuinely surprised us.

1. The average plate is lighter on protein than people think

Across all 1,530 dishes, the average serving carries 271 calories and 11.3g of protein. That sounds reasonable until you split it by diet. Vegetarian dishes average just 6.9g of protein per serving. To hit a typical daily protein target, that is a lot of katoris.

A vegetarian Indian thali is rarely short on calories. It is very often short on protein.

This is the quiet problem behind a lot of Indian diets. The food is satisfying and carb-rich, so hunger is handled, but protein quietly falls behind. It is not a failure of the cuisine. It is a planning gap, and it is fixable once you can actually see the numbers per dish.

2. The regional protein map

Protein per serving is not spread evenly across the country. Western India leads, helped by a mix of coastal fish, peanuts and protein-heavy legume dishes. Central India trails, though our sample there is smaller.

3. The surprise: the Northeast eats the most protein per calorie

If you rank dishes by protein density, grams of protein for every 100 calories, the Northeast runs away with it. The leaders are not heavy curries. They are lean, fermented, lightly oiled plates built around fish and pork with bamboo shoot.

Masor Tenga from Assam delivers 25g of protein in 178 calories. Naga pork with bamboo shoot and the smoked, chilli-forward dishes of Nagaland and Mizoram sit right behind it, often above 14g of protein per 100 calories. Very little oil, a lot of protein, plenty of flavour. It is one of the most quietly nutritious regional cuisines in the country, and almost nobody talks about it that way.

4. Where vegetarians actually find protein

The 6.9g average for vegetarian dishes hides the high performers. Paneer, soy, and the legume-and-grain combinations of a full thali do most of the heavy lifting.

The lesson is not "eat more paneer". It is that protein in a vegetarian diet comes from stacking sources: a dal, a paneer or soy dish, and a legume on the side add up far faster than any single bowl.

5. The fibre champions are all chickpeas

Fibre tells a cleaner story. The highest-fibre dishes in the dataset are, almost without exception, built on chana. Chana masala with roti tops the list at 24g of fibre. Undhiyu, a Kerala sadya, chole, and dal makhani all clear 18g. If you want fibre and plant protein in the same plate, the humble chickpea is hard to beat.

What we take from this

Indian food is not unhealthy. It is unmeasured. The same plate can be a protein win or a protein gap depending on which two dishes you put on it, and almost no one can see that difference while they are eating. That is the entire reason we built Svas: not to swap your food for salads, but to show you the real numbers behind the food you already love, so a few small choices add up.

Method: figures are per-serving macros computed from IFCT-2017 as the sum of each ingredient at its standard home-recipe portion, then dietitian-reviewed. Sample of 1,530 dishes tagged to a specific state across 28 states and 6 regions. Combo plates include their rice or roti, which is why thali servings show higher absolute protein. Figures are estimates and vary with portion and preparation.
